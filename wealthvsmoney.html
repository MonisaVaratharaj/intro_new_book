Wealth vs Money and Inflation Impact



Wealth vs Money

Money is not the same as wealth.

Money is how we transfer wealth. Money is social credits. It is the ability to have credits and debits of other people’s time. If I do my job right, if I create value for society, society says, “Oh, thank you. We owe you something in the future for the work you did in the past. Here’s a little IOU. Let’s call that money.”

Wealth is the thing you want. Wealth is assets that earn while you sleep. Wealth is the factory, the robots, cranking out things.

Wealth is the computer program that’s running at night, serving other customers. Wealth is even money in the bank that is being reinvested into other assets, and into other businesses. Even a house can be a form of wealth, because you can rent it out, although that’s probably a lower productivity use of land than some commercial enterprise.

So, my definition of wealth is much more businesses and assets that can earn while you sleep, while you eat, while you play golf—simply we say: wealth generates money even when you're not actively involved.

Let’s Say You Earn ₹1,000 Per Month

Let’s assume you earn ₹1,000 per month, save ₹500, and spend ₹500 (taken for simplicity) – we are going to calculate the tax and inflation rate.

What is the Inflation Rate?

The inflation rate is the percentage increase in the prices of goods and services over a specific period, typically measured annually. It reflects the decline in the purchasing power of money—meaning that over time, you can buy fewer goods with the same amount of money.

For example, if the inflation rate is 6%, an item that costs ₹100 today would cost ₹106 next year.

Why Does Inflation Happen?

Inflation occurs due to various economic factors, broadly categorized as demand-pull inflation, cost-push inflation, or built-in inflation. We are not focusing on this, let's calculate only the inflation rate.

Step-by-Step Calculation

Step 1: Income Tax Calculation

Assume you're under the new tax regime in India for FY 2023-24.

Tax Slabs (New Regime):

  • ₹0 to ₹3 lakhs: No tax
  • ₹3 lakhs to ₹6 lakhs: 5% of the income exceeding ₹3 lakhs
  • ₹6 lakhs to ₹9 lakhs: 10% of the income exceeding ₹6 lakhs
  • ₹9 lakhs to ₹12 lakhs: 15% of the income exceeding ₹9 lakhs
  • ₹12 lakhs to ₹15 lakhs: 20% of the income exceeding ₹12 lakhs
  • Above ₹15 lakhs: 30% of the income exceeding ₹15 lakhs

Since ₹12,000 annually is well below ₹3,00,000, you pay zero income tax.

Step 2: Post-Tax Income

Since there is no tax deduction:

Monthly Post-Tax Income: ₹1,000 (no tax is deducted from ₹1,000)

Step 3: Savings and Inflation Impact

You save ₹500 per month, which totals ₹6,000 annually. If you deposit this in a bank savings account earning 4% interest:

Interest Earned: ₹6,000 × 4% = ₹240

Total Savings with Interest: ₹6,000 + ₹240 = ₹6,240

Adjusting for Inflation (6% annually):

Inflation reduces the purchasing power of money by 6%.

Value Lost Due to Inflation: ₹6,240 × 6% = ₹374.40

Real Value of Savings After Inflation: ₹6,240 – ₹374.40 = ₹5,865.60

Interpretation in Terms of Purchasing Power

Year 1: ₹6,240 could buy the full basket of goods.

Year 2: ₹6,240 is insufficient to buy the same basket of goods; the real value of money is now only ₹5,865.60. Even though your money is still ₹6,240, you can only purchase items worth ₹5,865 because your purchasing power has decreased over time.

The 6% inflation effectively reduced the purchasing power of your money, even though the savings amount remained unchanged.

Impact of Inflation Over Multiple Years

If inflation continues year after year, the purchasing power of your money will keep declining unless your savings grow at the same or higher rate.

Example:

  • Year 1 Real Value: ₹6,240
  • Year 2 Real Value: ₹5,865.60
  • Year 3 Real Value (assuming another 6% inflation): ₹5,865.60 × 0.94 = ₹5,513.66

This demonstrates how inflation eats away at the value of your money over time.

Insights: Building wealth and assets is essential to beat inflation and maintain or grow your purchasing power over time. Simply saving money in a low-interest bank account is often not enough, as inflation can erode its value.
